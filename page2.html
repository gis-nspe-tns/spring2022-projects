Subway Accessibility in NYC | Radhika Joshi

This project explores how accessible the NYC subway is for those who live in New York. I explored two dimensions: accessibility for the general population and accessibility for the disabled population of the city. I mapped the population density per census tract of New York City, followed by subway stops. By creating a 0.3-mile isochrone around each of the subway stops, I was able to estimate the number of people who could reach the subway within approximately a 5-minute walk. I did the same with wheelchair accessible stations, mapped onto the same population density. I found that the subway covers 69.2 sq miles with all stations and 20.6 sq miles with accessible stations. In my results, I explain why the census tracts served and populations therein are overestimated based on the spatial analysis methods chosen; however a relative portrait of accessibly distribution is still achieved.

A GIS analysis approach to the Resource Curse Theory | Molly Simpson

This project undertakes a spatial assessment of the controversial ‘resource curse’ theory, which suggests that countries with an abundance of natural resources are more likely to experience a higher rate of economic turmoil. More recently, the theory has been extended to violence, in which it is suggested that NR abundance also correlates with higher rates of social ills in the form of conflict and violence. The primary objective of this project is to assess whether there are any clear spatial correlations between an area’s NR richness and reliance, and rates of conflict and violence. Analysis is conducted at continent level (Africa) and country-level (Uganda and Nigeria). Across both scales, there is only a weak positive correlation, indicating that - from these findings - there is limited evidence to support the theory in question. This project adds to a body of research that critically examines the relevance of the ‘resource curse’ theory.

Fortress Europe: Changes in Migration Flows to the European Union 2015 - 2019 | Isadora Jahanfar Tholin

Migration to the European Union has decreased dramatically since 2015. One major key to understanding why is the various deals the EU has negotiated with countries that have borders with the EU as well as with countries that have a large population of emigrants from their home country to the EU. In my GIS spatial analysis, I mapped the changes in migration flows to the EU from 2015 to 2019. In addition to mapping the overall flows, I also looked closer into migration flows from two regions: the Horn of Africa and Syria/Iraq. Some of the main findings in the analysis were (1) Migration flows to the EU has decreased but not vanished. People are still making the often dangerous journey to the EU. Nationals from countries in the Horn of Africa have seen a dramatic decrease of illegal border crossings since 2015. Nationals from Iraq and Syria have also - to a lesser extent - crossed the Eastern Mediterranean border line over to the EU since 2015.

Measuring School Sentiment using Survey Data | Mario Biancamano

Every year, NYC sends out surveys to teachers, students, and parents, asking them questions about how they view their schools in a variety of ways. This is part of the School Quality Report, which is designed to diagnose and remedy deficiencies. The five areas that are covered in the questionnaire are: Collaborative Teachers, Effective School Leadership, Rigorous Instruction, Strong Family-Community Ties, and Trust. This dataset was mapped, average scores were attained across all survey questions, then compared to per capita income data using a social vulnerability data. The survey data was broken down by school district. Unfortunately there are data gaps and a difficulty in a proper comparison between census tract geometry and school district geometry. Regardless, there does not seem to be a significant correlation between the survey data and the income data. Further analysis is needed to understand the correlation or lack thereof.
